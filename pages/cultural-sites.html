Ancient Rock Fortress

Sigiriya, a massive column of rock, vertically rises 200m from its labyrinths of inhospitable forested plains encompassing a water garden, boulder gardens, and terrace gardens. Located in the Matale District in the Central Province of Sri Lanka, this fortress city is embellished with water gardens, boulder and cave gardens, terraced grass gardens, frescoes and a mirror wall which are derived as a resultant of the profusion of Sinhalese civilization mingled with the long cherished artistic, archeological, architectural, historical and cultural values. It is also renowned for the mesmeric vistas across the land.

Historical Background

The historical roots of Sigiriya run back to the third century BC when this place served for the Buddhist monks as a rock-shelter mountain monastery. During the time of 477-495CE, this place served as the dwelling for King Kassapa, who was born to one of the wives of King Dhatusena, the first king of the Moriyan dynasty. Kassapa rebelled against his father for declaring Moggallana, the son of the most favored queen of the king, as the heir to the throne. This political mayhem articulated with Kassapa consolidating power drove Moggallana away to India, but he vowed to return from India and reclaim the throne.

Inspired by his cherished desire of creating his version of earthly paradise coincided with the intention of making preparations for the expected invasion from Moggallana, the rightful heir to the throne, Kassapa orchestrated his abode as a perdurable fortress with defensive structures. This ancient citadel complex is identified to be the oldest as well as the best example for ancient city planning, water engineering. Palace and other structures on this fortress city as well as the structures surrounding the Sigiriya rock were declared as a world heritage by the UNESCO in 1982.

The pleasure-dome of king Kashyapa is designed based on a fixed axial modular geometric plan, with a straight open path stretching from the entrance towards the rock in an imaginary east-west axis around which the whole city is built upon. There are two moats and several dams around Sigiriya rock to ensure security.

Sigiriya Water Garden

Clinging to the moats is a water garden in which the first section is fashioned gathering four pools, centering a small island, with pathways running towards the surrounding gardens. Beyond this area lies a lovely fountain encompassing ponds and channels nourished by a serpentine-shaped little river. The hydraulics system which was designed to circulate water to the lakes and water fountains is said to be built in the fifth century, perhaps the world's oldest complex water circulation system.

This complex drainage system consists of surface and underground canals, locks, lakes, bridges, fountains and underground as well as surface water pumps. It is said to perfectly equalize the ponds and prevent flooding on the rock top in the rainy season. Demonstrating the hydraulic sophistication of the ancient architect, even after a thousand and five hundred years from their initial construction, these fountains proudly gush water in their original form, making the travelers enthralled.

Several Other Rock Shelters

The main path leads to the boulder garden which spreads across the area that follows after the water garden. Dispersed across Sigiri forested plains, there are approximately twenty rock shelters which have been serving for the monks as monastery buildings even before Kassapa's era. They provide an impressive sight for the explorers. This garden lacks the symmetrical structure prevailing in the water garden and holds an asymmetrical structure as it is shaped around the rock formations which were existing.

The Deraniyagala cave comes into the vicinity shortly after ascending through the garden. It protects the remaining faded damsels within its stone walls and the well-preserved dripstone ledges. Preaching Rock containing tiered platforms believed to have been used by orating monks while Asana Chapel has served as a meeting hall for monks. Cobra Hood Rock is a little deviated from the main path and has been named due to the Archaic Brahmi script inscription on the ledge dated to the second century BC.

Sigiriya Frescoes

Sigiri frescoes are the most iconic and mostly celebrated non-religious paintings which demonstrate the prowess of the ancient Sri Lankan artistry at its zenith. At a short distance from the base, about 100 meters above ground, there lie two spiral cases which lead towards a sheltered cave. This cave once held the reputation for being the largest art gallery in the world, gracing an area of 140m in length and 40m in height.

In this cave domicile, the most illustrious Sigiri Apsara (damsels) epitomize womanly beauty with their sinuous bodies, voluptuous bosoms and full lips blended with vibrant, fluid touches. Even though the exact identities of these damsels are unclear, many believe them to be King Kassapa's consorts of the harem. The most significant validation presented to prove this view is the three circled tattoo worn on the necks of these damsels, indicative of them being owned by the king. Another convincing theory by modern art historians is that these damsels portray celestial nymphs floating amongst the clouds while showering human beings with flowers.

Mirror Wall

Further along the Sigiri frescoes, halfway along the vertical western side of the rock is a wide pathway, with a two-meter high mirror-walled outer edge, connecting the north and the south. This highly polished white masonry parapet wall, known as the ‘Katapath Paura,' reflects the frescoes on the opposite wall. Invigorated by the overwhelming beauty of this place and the seductive damsels, travelers during the 8th and 14th centuries inscribed their deep-felt musings on the mirror wall, pouring their emotions into letters. More than 600 graffitis have been deciphered by archaeologists, demonstrating the fascination this place has evoked in visitors. The following graffiti is an excellent piece of art manifesting the staggering beauty of King Kassapa's dwelling.

"Hail! I am vira-vidura bato [and] I wrote this. The gentle breeze blew — the breeze which is wet with cool dew drops — taking (with it) fragrant perfume, in the spring sunshine, the jasmine and the water-lily, being adorned with flowers shone all over. Leaving it (all aside), my mind was agitated on seeing these golden colored ones and, being shot at, whilst going, by their sidelong glances, I became prostrate on the slope of this rock. Having seen to my heart's content, the five hundred damsels on the surface of the rock, and having well remembered one (of them), Heaven (itself) does not take my mind." (Panique.com.au, 2018)

The direct exposure to natural circumstances for over a thousand and five hundred years, with the passage of time, has led to the two ends of the mirror wall—connecting the southern end to the terraced gardens and the northern end to the lion platform—being replaced with two steel bridges.

Lions Gate Sigiriya

Right below the summit stand two massive lion paws, the only remnants of an enormous lion statue symbolizing Sinhalese royalty and pride. The staircase, starting at the lion's feet, goes around its massive body and finally enters its mouth. Some historians argue that the fortress city was built during the reign of an ancient king who lived long before King Kassapa. They contend that the paws beside the entrance are not lion paws—since a lion's paw typically has four fingers with one hidden—but instead, these gigantic paws have three fingers with protruding claws.

The argument, proclaimed by these historians, suggests a mythological creature known as "Gurula," featuring bird legs, massive wings fixed to a human body with four human arms and an eagle head. They believe that this ancient city fortress was initially built by a king who placed Gurula in a venerable elevation, and that King Kassapa later adorned the site aesthetically. Thus, these massive paws are seen as resembling the paws of a Gurula rather than those of a lion.

Breathtaking Views From Sigiriya

On top of the rock lies a well-fortified citadel overlooking the area, its gardens and pools captivating travelers with astonishing vistas across a forested canopy. The slanting shaped summit, constructed with advanced engineering and architectural technology, stands roughly 200m above the ground and is divided into three distinct elevated areas.

Sigiriya is undoubtedly a sight that one’s eyes should not miss. From the moment you begin your journey until you reach the peak, you experience a wide range of emotions—overwhelming happiness derived from the beauty of this fortress city, coupled with the fear and anxiety of climbing a steel staircase attached to a sheer rock.

Dambulla Cave Temple

Nestled in the heart of Sri Lanka, the Dambulla Cave Temple stands as a testament to the country's rich cultural and religious heritage. This UNESCO World Heritage Site, also known as the Golden Temple of Dambulla, has fascinated travelers, historians, and spiritual seekers from around the globe. But what makes this ancient complex so special? Let's take a deep dive into the mysteries and marvels of Dambulla Cave Temple.

The temple complex consists of five main caves, each filled with an impressive collection of statues and paintings depicting the life of Buddha and various Hindu deities. These artworks, some of which date back over 2,000 years, are a testament to the artistic and spiritual fervor of the region's ancient inhabitants. The largest cave, known as Maharaja Viharaya, houses a magnificent 14-meter-long reclining Buddha statue, surrounded by intricate murals and smaller statues that tell stories of devotion and reverence.

In addition to its religious significance, the Dambulla Cave Temple offers stunning views of the surrounding landscape. From its perch on a rocky hill, visitors can gaze out over verdant forests and distant mountains, making the journey up the temple's many steps well worth the effort. Whether you're drawn to its spiritual aura, historical importance, or natural beauty, the Dambulla Cave Temple is a destination that promises to leave a lasting impression on all who visit.

A Glimpse into Dambulla Cave Temple History

The Dambulla Cave Temple dates back to the 1st century BCE. King Valagamba of Anuradhapura transformed these natural caves into a magnificent temple, showcasing a unique blend of history, art, and architecture. The site consists of five main caves, each adorned with intricate murals and over 150 statues of Buddha, reflecting the rich cultural heritage of ancient Sri Lanka. The caves are situated on a massive rock, offering stunning panoramic views of the surrounding landscape. The site has been a pilgrimage destination for over 22 centuries, making it one of the oldest and best-preserved cave temples in Sri Lanka, and a testament to the enduring spiritual devotion of its visitors.

Dambulla Five Main Caves

The complex consists of five main caves, each one uniquely adorned with intricate frescoes and statues that depict various scenes from Buddhist mythology. These caves serve as a sacred space for Buddhist worship and house over 150 statues of Buddha in different poses and expressions. The artistry and craftsmanship evident in these caves are truly awe-inspiring, showcasing the dedication and skill of the ancient artisans who created them. Additionally, the surrounding area offers a serene atmosphere with lush greenery and picturesque views, making it an ideal destination for those seeking spiritual and artistic inspiration.

  1. Cave of the Divine King

    The first cave, known as Devaraja Lena or "Cave of the Divine King," features an impressive 47-foot-long reclining Buddha statue, which is intricately carved and beautifully painted. This statue is surrounded by numerous smaller figures and elaborate murals that depict various scenes from the Buddha's life. This cave is a perfect introduction to the religious significance and artistic brilliance of the temple complex, showcasing the devotion and craftsmanship of its creators. Visitors often find themselves in awe of the serene atmosphere and the historical richness that emanates from every corner of this sacred space.

  2. Cave of the Great Kings

    Next is Maharaja Lena or "Cave of the Great Kings." Here, you'll find statues of both Buddha and two ancient Sri Lankan kings, which stand as a testament to the country's rich heritage. The walls are covered with vibrant frescoes depicting significant events from Buddha's life, each painting a masterpiece of spiritual and historical significance. These artworks not only tell the story of Buddha's journey but also reflect the artistry and devotion of the ancient craftsmen who created them. Visitors can spend hours marveling at the intricate details and absorbing the serene ambiance of this sacred space.

  3. The Great New Monastery

    The third cave, Maha Alut Viharaya or "The Great New Monastery," is the largest of all five. Renovated in the 18th century, this cave showcases a stunning array of Buddha statues and beautifully preserved frescoes that depict various scenes from Buddhist mythology. The intricate details in the frescoes and the serene expressions on the statues reflect the high level of artistry and devotion that went into creating this sacred space. Visitors often find themselves in awe of the spiritual atmosphere and the historical significance of this remarkable cave.

  4. The Western Cave Temple

    Pachima Viharaya, also known as the "Western Cave Temple," is the fourth cave in the complex and, although smaller in size, it is equally captivating. This serene spot houses several exquisitely crafted Buddha statues, each with intricate details that reflect the skill of ancient artisans. Offering a quieter and more intimate setting, Pachima Viharaya is ideal for those seeking moments of reflection and prayer away from the bustling crowds. The peaceful ambiance and the spiritual aura of this cave make it a hidden gem worth exploring.

  5. Second New Monastery

    The fifth cave, or Devana Alut Viharaya, known as the "Second New Monastery," is relatively newer compared to the others in the complex. This cave stands out because it encapsulates a unique blend of both historical and contemporary Buddhist art, offering visitors a distinct experience. Inside, you'll find intricately designed frescoes and statues that reflect a fusion of ancient techniques with modern artistic expressions, making it a remarkable site for both art enthusiasts and spiritual seekers alike.

Frescoes of Dambulla

The walls and ceilings of the caves are adorned with over 21,000 square feet of intricate religious frescoes. These stunning paintings vividly depict scenes from Buddha's life, illustrating his birth, enlightenment, and various significant moments. Additionally, they feature numerous Jataka tales, which are stories of Buddha's previous lives. These frescoes serve as a visual narrative of Buddhist teachings, chronicling the spiritual journey and moral lessons, while also providing a glimpse into the rich history and cultural heritage of Sri Lanka. The artistry and detail in these frescoes offer a captivating insight into the devotional practices and artistic traditions of the ancient Sri Lankan civilization.

Statues Galore Dambulla

The Dambulla Cave Temple is home to over 150 statues, including remarkable representations of Vishnu and Ganesha, which highlight the syncretic nature of Sri Lankan Buddhism. These statues vary in size, with the largest being over 15 meters long. The temple itself is a complex of five caves, each adorned with intricate murals and paintings that depict various facets of Buddhist history and mythology. Visitors are often struck by the serene atmosphere and the temple's rich cultural heritage, making it a significant pilgrimage site for both locals and tourists alike.

Pilgrimage Destination

For centuries, Dambulla has been a significant pilgrimage site for Buddhists, attracting devotees from all corners of the world. Many visit the temple to pay their respects and seek spiritual solace, drawn by its historical importance and religious reverence. The serene atmosphere and the sacred aura make it an ideal place for meditation and reflection, offering a peaceful retreat from the hustle and bustle of everyday life. The temple complex, adorned with intricate carvings and ancient paintings, stands as a testament to the rich cultural heritage and spiritual significance of the region.

Architectural Marvel

The temple complex is not just a spiritual hub but also an architectural marvel. The ingenious use of natural rock formations, combined with human craftsmanship, creates a harmonious blend of nature and culture. Visitors are often awestruck by the intricate carvings that adorn the temple walls, each telling a story of ancient traditions and beliefs. The serene environment, set amidst lush greenery and tranquil surroundings, provides a perfect backdrop for meditation and reflection, making it a haven for both spiritual seekers and architecture enthusiasts alike.

Conservation Efforts

Over the years, numerous conservation efforts have been undertaken to preserve this historical site. UNESCO has played a crucial role in maintaining the integrity and authenticity of the Dambulla Cave Temple.

How to Get There

Getting to Dambulla is relatively easy. It is well-connected by road, and public transport options are available. The nearest major city is Kandy, which is about 72 kilometers away.

Address - Kandy - Jaffna Hwy, Dambulla, Sri Lanka

Best Time to Visit

The best time to visit Dambulla is during the dry season, from January to April. The weather is pleasant, making it easier to explore the temple complex without any hindrance.

What to Wear in Dambulla

Since Dambulla is a religious site, it is important to dress modestly to show respect for the culture and traditions. Wearing white is recommended as it symbolizes purity and reverence. Make sure to cover your shoulders and knees, as this is a common practice in many religious places to signify humility and deference. Additionally, wearing comfortable yet respectful clothing will ensure that you can explore the site without any discomfort. It is also advisable to remove your shoes before entering certain areas, so be prepared for that as well.

Temple of the Tooth

Located in Kandy, the Temple of the Tooth is a revered Buddhist temple that houses the sacred relic of the Buddha’s tooth. This temple is not only a symbol of Sri Lanka’s spiritual heritage but also a focal point of religious devotion and cultural celebrations.

Discover the Spiritual Heritage

The Temple of the Tooth stands as a beacon of spiritual reverence, drawing pilgrims and visitors from across the globe. The temple complex is a living museum of Buddhist art and architecture, reflecting centuries of religious traditions and cultural influences.

The Sacred Relic and Its Significance

At the heart of the temple lies the sacred relic—a tooth of the Buddha—believed to embody the spiritual essence of his teachings. This relic is venerated as a symbol of enlightenment and is central to the religious ceremonies held throughout the year.

Historical Background

The origins of the Temple of the Tooth date back to the 4th century, and over the centuries, it has been rebuilt and expanded to reflect changing artistic and architectural styles. The temple has witnessed numerous historical events, from invasions to periods of cultural renaissance, making it an enduring testament to the resilience of Buddhist traditions in Sri Lanka.

Architectural Splendor

The temple is an architectural marvel that combines traditional Kandyan design with intricate carvings and vibrant murals. Its ornate structure, lush gardens, and serene courtyards provide a peaceful setting for meditation and reflection, while the main shrine exudes a sense of grandeur and divine beauty.

Cultural and Spiritual Significance

Beyond its physical beauty, the Temple of the Tooth plays a crucial role in the cultural and spiritual life of Sri Lanka. It is a focal point for major religious festivals, including the famed Esala Perahera—a grand procession that features traditional dancers, drummers, and decorated elephants. This festival reinforces the temple’s status as a symbol of national identity and devotion.

Rituals and Festivals

Daily pujas and elaborate ceremonies at the temple honor the sacred relic and offer visitors a glimpse into the deep-rooted traditions of Sri Lankan Buddhism. These rituals not only highlight the spiritual atmosphere of the temple but also provide an immersive cultural experience.

Practical Tips for Visitors

When visiting the Temple of the Tooth, dress modestly to show respect—ensure that shoulders and knees are covered. Remove your shoes before entering the temple complex and consider joining a guided tour to gain deeper insights into the temple’s history and significance.

A Journey of Enlightenment

A visit to the Temple of the Tooth is more than just a tour—it’s a journey into the heart of Sri Lanka’s spiritual heritage. Whether you are a pilgrim seeking solace, a history enthusiast, or a curious traveler, the temple promises an experience that will leave you inspired and enlightened.

Ancient City Anuradhapura

Anuradhapura is one of the most visited cities in Sri Lanka by local as well as foreign pilgrims. This city is located in the North Central Province, Anuradhapura District. It marks one of the cornerstones of the Sri Lankan cultural triangle and is renowned for its ruins, monuments that are renovated, historical sites and other constructions with an archeological value. This city had been proudly serving as the capital of the country for a large number of years and unraveled thousands of hidden mysteries of the Sri Lankan civilization. Because of its significant importance religiously, culturally and historically, it was declared as a world heritage by UNESCO.

History of Anuradhapura

Anuradhapura remained as the capital city of Sri Lanka from the fourth century AD until the eleventh century AD. This city was one of the most strongholds and stable cities among the other capitals of South Asia. There were several instances where the invaders from other countries, especially from India usurped the city. However, the kings with the never-ceasing support from the Buddhist monks, coincided with the courage and the boldness of the Sinhalese people, fearlessly fought against these invaders and reunited the country.

Anuradhapura is an era when the architecture, craftsmanship and water engineering was at its zenith. This is well demonstrated through the irrigation system prevailed at the time. Even today, after many thousands of years elapsed from its origin, these reservoirs survive and function well and nourish the thousands of paddy fields which provide rice for the whole nation.

Best Time to Go

It is tough to pinpoint the best time to visit Anuradhapura as it is a place visited by tourists almost all the time of the year. There prevails a hot temperature in Anuradhapura. If you prefer to glimpse Anuradhapura during the warmest season, you can choose the months May, June, and July. Among these months the warmness is at its best at the latter part of the month May.

Preparation For The Visit And Dress Code

Anuradhapura is one of the most celebrated cities in Sri Lanka, and it holds a history which runs back to centuries. Its religious value is unmatched, and it is home for many of the sacred places in the island. Thus, your dress code is of paramount importance. You must not wear shorts or sleeveless clothes.

It is essential that you cover your legs and shoulders. You can wear a sarong to cover your legs and a scarf to cover your shoulders. You can take them off once you pass the temple or the religious place, but it would be burdensome as there are so much of places to visit. In case you forget to bring them, there are spots by the sacred places where you can hire one of them. However, if you are concerned about your hygiene, you would not choose this option. It would be great to wear something simple which covers your legs and shoulders. Light colored clothes are the most appreciated and welcome in this ancient city. It would be an excellent indication of your respect and admiration for the long cherished history of Anuradhapura.

Places To Visit In Anuradhapura

Ruwanwelisaya

Realizing the prophecy made by Arahant Mahinda Thero, who was sent to spread Buddhism in Sri Lanka as a missionary by the great emperor Ashoka in India, King Dutugemunu, the peerless hero who defeated the greatest Indian invader of all time, king Elara who ruled Sri Lanka for 45 years, built Ruwanwelisaya. Known as Maha Stupa, Swarnamali Mahasaya and Rathnamali Chethiya, Ruwanwelisaya rises towards the sky blending and harmonizing the human world and the celestial worlds. This one of the finest creations of the Sri Lankan history which well demonstrates the prowess of the ancient artists fused with the astounding mathematical and architectural knowledge.

For Buddhist, who live throughout the world, Ruwanwelisaya is venerated as the pinnacle of Buddhism. It is mainly because this Stupa contains the larges portion of the relics of Lord Buddha and according to the ancient chronicles its one-eighth of the relics. Being one of the main benefactors of Buddhism, King Dutugemunu was able to bring relics even from India. After depositing these relics, they made the largest stupa ever imaginable. It is 300 feet in height, and its diameter is 370 feet. After the Great War between himself and King Elara, King Dutugemunu was economically unable to patronize such a huge construction. However, his heart filled with devotion compelled the king of the gods, god Shaka to aid him with the material support. Thus he appointed god Vishwakarma to provide king Dutugemunu with the materials needed. Later all the required material appeared miraculously in the neighboring localities.

There are four entrances to the sand compound and among the main entrance is the one you find on the Eastern side. Once you enter for this entrance, you can see two vases which are called “Punkalasa” with two lion figures standing by them. There is also a wall with 338 elephant figures engraved in it. Once you enter the stone podium of which the floor is designed, you will be able to see four small stupas situated in four directions worshipping the massive stupa standing at the middle demonstrating the power of Buddhism to guide the human beings towards the supreme bliss of Nibbana.

It is wonderful how the ancient architect has embedded religious theories in its structure. This great Stupa takes the shape of a water bubble. The shape of the Stupa encompasses a sacred meaning, a meaning of impermanence. Then there is the pinnacle with a glistening crest gem made of crystals. This gem symbolizes the ultimate realization of Buddhism, the “Nirvana.”

The shrine room found in Ruwanwelisaya is famous for its uniqueness decorated with a Wahalkada where you can find engravings of lions, tusk elephants, horses, cattle, lotuses, and the “Kalpa Wruksha” (a divine tree which fulfills your desires ). Moreover, once you pass them, you would see the remainings of the paintings which once have lightened up the whole place. Inside the shrine room, there are four Buddha statues representing Kakusandha, Konagama, Kasyapa, and Gautama. There is another statue representing the Maithree Bodhisattva. The most significant monument in this shrine room is the footprint of the Buddha which is done in marble with the 108 auspicious marks. Two other Buddha statues are representing the “Piriniwan Manchakaya” ( last moment of the Buddha ) and the other one carved in granite in a seated posture. Two statues of King Dutugemunu and King Elara who has once been his rival can be seen in this shrine room.

Mirisawetiya

Mirisawetiya stupa stands between the Basawkkulama Tank and Tissa Tank. Mirisawetiya is the first Stupa done under the patronage of King Dutugemunu. According to the folklore, this name was fashioned after a mistake done by the king. King Dutugemunu keeping the promise to his father, offered the first portion of his every meal to Buddhist monks. One day mistakenly he dined without offering meals. It was a “Miris” (chili) curry. As compensation to his grave mistake, he built a stupa and named it Mirisawetiya.

Because of the severe droughts and various illnesses, Sri Lankan kings had to shift the kingdom to Polonnaruwa. This influenced severely on all the religious constructions in Anuradhapura with the loss of kings’ patronage for their betterment. Mirisa Wetiya because of the state of negligence reached its decadence. Later during the British Era, fascinated by the unique Wahalkada, they started to find the lost construction. Even after the British, Sri Lankan politicians who came into power continued with the digging process. Finally, they restored the old Mirisa Wetiya. However, the night before the opening the Stupa collapsed. During the period of Ranasinghe Premadasa, a former president, the Stupa was renovated. With the contribution of all the politicians today Mirisa Wetiya shines as one of the most magnificent stupas in Sri Lanka.

Sri Ma Bodhi Tree

The oldest tree with a recorded date of the plantation is Sri Ma Bodhi tree which is planted in Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka. The history of this holy tree runs back to the 288 BCF. Venerable Sangamitta Therani brought this to Sri Lanka accepting the request from Venerable Mahinda Thero, the Buddhist missionary who came to spread Buddhism in the country. From that day onwards until today this sacred tree has been subjected to veneration by all the Buddhists scattered around the world. In the history, there were instances where separate groups were assigned for the protection of this tree from outsiders. That is a clear indication of the importance of the tree.

Your behavior should not cause any harm to the respectability of the sacred tree. Starting from your attire to the way you talk, your respect for this holy and most venerated tree should be demonstrated.

Abhayagiriya

Abhayagiriya was a stupa constructed under the patronage of King Walagamba who was also known as King Wattagamini Abhaya. Walagamba is the king who freed the country from Indian usurpers who ruled the country for so many years.

According to the descriptions of the monk Fa-Hsien, once upon a time this gigantic stupa was decorated with gold, silver and thousands of jewels. But as a results of centuries of negligence this place became deteriorated. But few years back the Archeological Department of Sri Lanka started restoring this stupa to its former glamour.

The name of this stupa has an interesting background story. When the King Walagamba was fleeing from the usurpers, a Hindu monk called “Giri” yelled “The great black Sinhala king is fleeing”. At that moment the king did not bother to do anything about this insulting comment but it got stamped in his mind. Later on, after defeating the invaders, king returned to this place and smashed the whole place to the ground and built a stupa on its ruins. Combining a part of his own name “Abhaya” and the Hindu monk “Giri” he named this stupa as Abhayagiriya.

Lowamahapaya

The vast area between the Ruwanwelisaya and the Jaya Sri Ma Bodhi Tree is covered with thousands of stone pillars. These are the only remainings of a great nine-story construction served as a Buddhist monastery. In the second century BC, this massive construction was built and donated by the great King Dutugemunu for the betterment of Buddhism. This place is also known as the Brazen Palace or the Loha Prasada. The name "Brazen Palace" was given as a result of the Bronze that was utilized for the roofing. The archeological knowledge in the past invested in this great work amazes even the modern archeologists. According to the beliefs of the archeologists a refectory, a chapter house and an assembly hall have been included in this monument. Today there is only a small chapter house which is being utilized by the monks. Unfortunately, the original construction cannot be witnessed today.

Thuparamaya Dagaba

Thuparamaya holds a special place in the hearts of the Buddhists as it is the first stupa built after the introduction of Buddhism in Sri Lanka by King Devanampiyatissa. This stupa is such an invaluable place which enshrines the sacred right collar bone of the Lord Buddha which was a gift from India celebrating the years of friendship. This stupa was built according to the guidance of the Buddhist missionary Arahat Mahinda Thero who came to Sri Lanka with the intention of spreading Buddhism in the country. The “Watadageya,” the stupa house characterizes this stupa as one of the rare manifestations of craftsmanship. Even though all that remain in this place are four concentric circles of stone pillars around the stupa, the grace and beauty of this whole place is still present. Once this stupa had been decorated with gold and silver. And there was a Stupa house (watadageya) protecting the stupa and it was built with golden bricks. This was further protected with golden doors. Later the South Indian invaders who were known as Pandyans destroyed the whole place and robbed all the wealth.

Samadhi Buddha Statue

Made out of dolomite marble, Samadhi Buddha Statue captures of the Lord Buddha’s never-ending kindness in the marble. The statue is 7 feet and 3 inches is in height. The most astonishing fact about the statue is that when you look at the statue in three different angles you see completely different features. When you look from the left you will see a sadness evoked from the features, when looked from the right you can see a slight smile and when looked from the front the features are neutral. Thus, Samadhi Buddha Statue is a monument which you must not ever miss when you are visiting Anuradhapura.

Ritigala Forest Monastery

Ritigala is an eco-tourist destination with a cultural as well as a religious value. This is the highest mountain in the north-central province. It stands in the midst of the dry plains of Anuradhapura, 766 meters above this sea level. The vegetation and the animals inhabit in Ritigala make the place rich in natural diversity. Wild elephants, bears, and even leopards can be spotted in the area. There are 70 caves in Ritigala, and the monks have used them from a very long time. There are inscriptions which indicate that this place had been a monastery retreat for hermit monks. This place also named as “Aritta Pabbatha” and was also being used by the kings as a hiding place from the Indian invaders. From the foot of the mountain, a short climb will take you to an area of 120 acres where the ruins are dispersed across the domain. Another notable feature of this place is the meditation platforms and the walkways which have been built without any disturbance to the natural flow of the vegetation. To enter the Strict Nature Reserve which has the most spellbinding views, you need to get permission from the Forest Department prior to your visit.

Polonnaruwa Ancient City

Polonnaruwa is a well-preserved ancient city that once served as the capital of Sri Lanka. Today, its impressive ruins of palaces, temples, and statues stand as a testament to the island’s rich history and artistic achievements.

A Glimpse into History

Established as the capital in the 11th century, Polonnaruwa was a thriving center of culture and governance. The city flourished under successive kings who invested in large-scale infrastructure, water management systems, and grandiose monuments. Its history is a fascinating blend of triumphs and challenges, reflecting the dynamic changes that have shaped Sri Lanka over the centuries.

Architectural Marvels

The ruins of Polonnaruwa reveal extraordinary examples of ancient engineering and architecture. Visitors can admire the remains of royal palaces, intricately carved Buddha statues, and grand stupas that hint at a bygone era of sophistication. Notable structures include the Royal Palace, Gal Vihara—a rock-cut sculpture of Buddha in meditation—and various stupas that exhibit remarkable craftsmanship.

Cultural and Religious Significance

Polonnaruwa is not only an archaeological treasure but also a living cultural heritage site. The city reflects the fusion of Buddhist influences with indigenous traditions, evident in its temple complexes and statuary. Pilgrims and history enthusiasts alike are drawn to the site, which continues to inspire awe with its serene atmosphere and profound spiritual legacy.

Exploring the City

Walking through Polonnaruwa is like stepping back in time. The extensive network of roads and water canals, still functional in parts, highlights the ancient engineers' expertise in urban planning. As you wander among the ruins, you'll discover hidden courtyards, ancient inscriptions, and beautifully restored monuments that narrate stories of a glorious past.

Modern Relevance and Conservation

Today, Polonnaruwa continues to capture the imagination of visitors, historians, and scholars. Efforts by the Archaeological Department and international organizations have ensured that the site remains well-preserved. Sustainable tourism and conservation initiatives help maintain the delicate balance between historical preservation and modern visitor needs.

Restoration projects are continually undertaken to safeguard the structural integrity of the ruins, while educational programs and guided tours provide visitors with insights into the ancient techniques and cultural significance of this magnificent city.

A Journey Through Time

A visit to Polonnaruwa is an immersive journey through time that reveals the artistic ingenuity and cultural richness of ancient Sri Lanka. Embracing the Legacy

Galle Fort

A coastal fortress that whispers tales of yesteryears, Galle Fort stands majestically, inviting history enthusiasts, architecture aficionados, and travel bloggers to explore its timeless charm. This ancient marvel, nestled in the heart of Sri Lanka, is a living testament to the island’s vibrant past and architectural prowess. Whether you are looking to uncover historical secrets, admire stunning construction, or document your travels, Galle Fort promises an unforgettable experience.

Unraveling the Mystique of Galle Fort

Galle Fort is a 16th-century fortification that has withstood the test of time. Built by the Portuguese and later fortified by the Dutch, this UNESCO World Heritage Site offers a blend of European architectural styles and South Asian traditions. But what makes Galle Fort so captivating?

The Architectural Brilliance of Galle Fort

The fort's architecture is a glorious fusion of European and Asian influences. Walking through its cobbled streets, you’ll be mesmerized by the colonial buildings, ancient churches, and quaint houses that tell stories of different epochs. The fort's well-preserved ramparts and bastions offer panoramic views of the Indian Ocean, creating an enchanting ambiance.

The Dutch Reformed Church, built in 1640, is a fine example of Dutch colonial architecture. Its simple yet elegant design, with a gabled roof and stained-glass windows, reflects the artistic sensibilities of the time. Another architectural gem is the Old Dutch Hospital, now transformed into a bustling shopping and dining precinct, showcasing adaptive reuse of historical structures.

A Walk Through History

Galle Fort is not just about bricks and mortar; it’s a living museum that narrates tales of conquests, trade, and cultural amalgamation. The fort’s history dates back to 1505 when the Portuguese arrived in Sri Lanka. They built the initial fortifications to protect the harbor from rival colonial powers. In 1640, the Dutch seized the fort and expanded its defenses, leaving an indelible mark on its architecture and layout.

The British took control in 1796, and though they made few changes to the fort, their influence is evident in the well-planned streets and administrative buildings. Each period of occupation added layers to Galle Fort’s rich tapestry, making it a microcosm of Sri Lanka’s colonial history.

Exploring the Fort’s Hidden Gems

While the fort’s ramparts and bastions are its most prominent features, Galle Fort is dotted with numerous hidden gems waiting to be discovered. The Maritime Archaeology Museum, housed in a historic warehouse, offers fascinating insights into Sri Lanka’s maritime history and the fort’s strategic importance in trade routes.

The Galle Lighthouse, standing tall since 1848, is the oldest lighthouse in Sri Lanka. It not only guides ships safely to the harbor but also offers breathtaking views of the coastline. The National Museum of Galle, located in a 1656 Dutch building, displays artifacts that showcase the region’s cultural heritage and colonial past.

The fort’s narrow streets are lined with boutique hotels, art galleries, and souvenir shops, making it a popular destination for travelers seeking both cultural enrichment and leisurely pursuits.
